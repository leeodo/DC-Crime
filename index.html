DC Crime Analysis

Group 33
Adiam Tesfaselassie   Rae Zhang   Yannian Liu   Zhuo Pan



Abstract

Washington, DC has had higher violent crime rates, when compared neighboring states Maryland and Virginia. As of recently, 2021 wraps up as one of the DC region’s most violent years in ages. Crime rates in Washington, DC vary by ward, where particular wards are impacted at a higher rate than others. Increasing violent crime rates harm the overall wellness of individuals and communities. Understanding what factors lead to the increase in violent crime it may be possible to reduce their frequency. To address this challenge, we propose building a data visualization that allows city administrators to visually explore a dataset of violent crimes committed by types of neighborhoods and explore what demographical, economical, and sociological factors are commonly associated with wards with those who experience higher rates of violent crime. This app will show the distribution of factors contributing to crimes committed and allow users to explore different aspects of this data by filtering and re-ordering different variables to compare factors that contribute to neighborhoods/ areas with high crime rates. We will focus on the different wards of Washington DC and will use some cities and counties in Maryland and Virginia.

Introduction

Crime is a significant concern for all, including city officials, businesses, everyday citizens, and other stakeholders. The prevalence of crime in a city often results in negative economic, political, environmental, and societal consequences that have lasting effects. As students living in Washington, DC, we're curious about crime rates in the newest city we reside in, Washington, DC. Using data from various datasets provided by the Washington, DC Metropolitan Police Department and multiple datasets on the OpenData DC website to better understand crime rates in Washington, DC, and neighboring states.


In general, data visualizations of crime are helpful for all stakeholders to predict crime risk, improve community relations, form budgets, allocate resources, and more. We believe that different groups will benefit from our data visualization. For example, those new to Washington, DC, will be able to use our visualizations to make informed decisions on their housing and ensure their safety. Similarly, business owners may be able to use our data visualization to better equip their business with better protective measures, such as investing in better insurance for their business. Our data visualizations are also helpful for policymakers within the Washington, DC government, who aim to decrease crime for the health and safety of community members. By providing different economic, social, and demographic characteristics of neighborhoods with high and low crime rates, we hope to highlight variables that directly or indirectly influence crime rates.


The project's main objective is to answer the following questions 1) what do crime rates look like in Washington, DC when compared to neighboring states, Maryland and Virginia throughout time? 2) What are the crime rates in Washington, DC? How do crime rates vary across neighborhoods and different wards in the city? 3) What are the most common types of crimes in Washington, DC? 4) What social, economic, and political characteristics correlate with the most common type of crime in Washington, DC?


To answer the questions listed above, our team used various interactive and non-interactive visualizations that city members, businesses, community members, and other stakeholders can utilize. We created visualizations assuming that our audience is groups who take a glance at data and do not have a technical background.

Data & Methods

Data Description

We used the following datasets to answer our questions: State Crime, DC crime dataset, demographic characteristics of DC by ward , economic characteristics of DC by ward, and housing characteristics of DC by ward. The description of each data is as follows:


  • State Crime: Based on the Unified Crime Reporting Statistics and the collaboration of the U.S. Department of Justice and FBI, this dataset provides the crime rates and totals for states across the U.S. from 1960 to 2019. The crime reports are categorized into property (Burglary, Larceny, Motor) and violent crime (Assault, Murder, Rape, Robbery). We will use the subset containing only the information about the DMV area, partially as a reference for the other datasets.
  • DC Crime dataset is an incident-based dataset. Every row is an incident that contains detailed time elements, geographic elements, and crime category elements. This data is made public by the Washington, DC Metropolitan police department. The whole dataset includes data as early as eight years ago. So it’s a very comprehensive dataset.

  • Demographic characteristics of DC by ward contains variables age, sex, race, ethnicity, total housing units, and voting age population by ward. This is a tidy data pulled from American Community Survey (ACS) 5-year data for 2020.

  • Economic characteristics of DC by ward contain variables to measure employment, commuting, occupation, income, health insurance, poverty, and more. This is a tidy data pulled from American Community Survey (ACS) 5-year data for 2020.

  • Housing characteristics of DC by ward contain variables on occupancy status, Units, Rooms, Year built, Owner/Renter (Tenure), Mortgage/Rent costs, and more. This is a tidy data pulled from American Community Survey (ACS) 5-year data for 2020.

Data Preprocessing

We preprocessed the data to make it easier to use. We used the following methods for each file:


DC Crime Dataset

  • After reading the data, the columns that are “datetime” type are converted to the corresponding type. Then data is aggregated by different types of tables for later use. This includes the number of crimes per day, the number of crimes per day by different types, the number of crimes per day per ward, and the number of violent crimes per ward in 2020, etc.

  • The latest data on social characteristics are in the year 2020, thus above tables were later filtered for the year 2020 only.

Demographic, Economic, Housing, and Social Characteristics Dataset of DC by ward

  • The four datasets are summarized in 8 wards and these data are later joined to existing datasets using the ward number.

DC Crime Map

This choropleth is meant to show the difference in the overall crime situation across eight wards in Washington, D.C.

The data was first aggregated to the total number of crimes happened in 2020 per ward and then the crime rate was derived. Lastly, the aggregated data has been merged with the geojson file.

The color of each ward is based on the absolute value of crimes. A legend of colormap is provided for reference. This map has a hover feature. When users hover their mouse over the map, users can see specific details of crime statistics.

DC Violent Crime Map

This choropleth is meant to show the difference in violent crime situations across eight wards in Washington, D.C.

The data was first filtered by crime type to pick out violent crimes only then aggregated to the total number of crimes happened in 2020 per ward. Then the crime rate was derived. Lastly, the aggregated data has been merged with the geojson file.

The color of each ward is based on the absolute value of crimes. A legend of colormap is provided for reference. This map has a hover feature. When users hover their mouse over the map, users can see specific details of crime statistics.

DC Marker Clusters Map

This visualization is meant to show a more detailed distribution of crimes happened in DC. This used the same data as the overall crime choropleth since it contains longitude and latitude.

The markers are added one by one and the leaflet package is smart enough to aggregate markers based on location.

The map has many circles zooming in and out. The number on the circle represents the number of crimes happened in the area. When users hover their mouse on a circle, it will show a polygon corresponding to the area the specific circle represents. Furthermore, the color of the circle is a gradient of numbers as green is low and orange is high.

DC Crime Heatmap

This heatmap is meant to show the density of crimes in Washington D.C., the folium heatmap function takes in location information as the marker cluster does. It automatically draws the density using color.

This map is continuous in the area and the density is represented by light orange(low) and red(high)
